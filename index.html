What is Thalassemia?

Thalassemia is a group of inherited blood disorders in which the body produces defective or less healthy red blood cells and hemoglobin. Thalassemia is genetic; it is single gene mutation that occurs on chromosome 11 or chromosome 16 depending on the type of thalassemia. It is a typically recessive disorder, though there have been rare exceptions of dominant inheritance reported in the past.

Symptoms include:

  • Fatigue
  • Weakness
  • Changes in skin color or yellowing of skin and eyes
  • Change or issues with facial bones
  • Slow growth
  • Swelling in the abdomen
  • Dark urine
  • Poor appetite

There are different types of thalassemia, primarily alpha-thalassemia and beta-thalassemia.


Alpha-thalassemia is the more common type of thalassemia, and it occurs when there is a problem with the alpha-globin chain due to mutations in the HBA1 or HBA2 genes on chromosome 16. Under alpha-thalassemia, there is alpha-thalassemia minor, which is a mild and often asymptomatic disorder, meaning the symptoms are not shown. Sometimes it can result in slight anemia. Hemoglobin H (HbH) disease is also a kind of alpha-thalassemia, which produces symptoms like jaundice, enlarged spleen, and bone complications. Finally there is alpha-thalassemia major, which is the more severe version of alpha-thalassemia, which can lead to death before birth (this is called hydrops fetalis). If a person has alpha-thalassemia, but none of the conditions stated above, then they are likely a silent carrier of the disorder

Beta-thalassemia occurs when there is a problem with the beta-globin chain due to mutations on the HBB gene on chromosome 11; the location the mutation occurs and the chain that it affects is the main difference between alpha-thalassemia and beta-thalassemia. Under beta-thalassemia, there is beta-thalassemia minor, which has the same symptoms as alpha-thalassemia. There is also beta-thalassemia intermedia, which causes mild or moderate anemia. It occurs when two mutated beta-globin genes are inherited, which results in a reduced amount of beta-globin chains. Finally there is beta-thalassemia major (A.K.A Cooley’s anemia). This is the most severe form of beta-thalassemia. This type of beta-thalassemia occurs when a person inherits two mutated beta-globin genes, similar to that of beta-thalassemia intermedia, but beta-thalassemia major results in little to no beta-globin production. People with this type of beta-thalassemia often need regular blood transfusion. For this reason, it is often called “transfusion-dependent thalassemia”.


How is thalassemia tested for? Thalassemia can be tested for through a few ways, like chorionic villus sampling, amniocentesis, a complete blood count test, and a hemoglobin test. Chronic villus sampling works by doctors removing a small portion of a mother’s placenta at the 11th week of her pregnancy, which is then taken and tested in a lab to find out if the baby will have thalassemia or not. Amniocentesis is also used to test for thalassemia, by taking fluid from around the unborn baby and checking the genes in the sample. A complete blood count, or a CBC, is when the doctor counts the number of red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets, while also measuring hemoglobin and hematocrit levels. This is often the first step when trying to test someone for thalassemia, and the individual may have to go through other tests like hemoglobin tests in order to receive a definite diagnosis.

Treatments and what their uses are

Treatment Destription
Blood Transfusions Primary way to treat thalassemia; provides red blood cells with healthy hemoglobin
Iron Chelation Therapy Removes the excess iron that can build up in the body due to regular blood transfusions
Blood and bone marrow transplant known as a hematopoietic stem cell transplant, replaces blood forming stem cells with healthy donar cells. (Only way to cure Thalassemia but it is rare to find a good match)
